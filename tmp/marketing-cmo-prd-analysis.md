## Presentation Strategy & Marketing Analysis for Hackathon Demo

**Reviewer:** Chief Marketing Officer perspective

## 1. Presentation Narrative -- Rating: 6/10

### What Works
The demo flow has a logical progression from "here's the data" to "here's the insight" to "here's the AI magic." The bookend of Season KPIs (big picture) leading to the Claude Advisor (conversational intelligence) is structurally sound. Starting with 239K transactions and $1.8M in revenue is a strong credibility opener -- it tells judges immediately that this is real data, not a toy.

### What's Missing: The Story Arc
The PRD treats this as a dashboard tour. Judges will see seven screens of charts. That is not a story. That is a software demo. The difference between winning and placing is narrative.

**The current arc:**
"Here's data. Here's more data. Here's a chart. Here's a heatmap. Here's a prediction. Here's an AI."

**The winning arc should be:**
"Here's a PROBLEM worth $120K/season. Here's how we FOUND it in the data. Here's how we SOLVE it with intelligence. Here's how a GM uses this TOMORROW MORNING to make money TONIGHT."

### Recommended Narrative Structure (10 minutes)

| Time | Beat | Emotional Note |
|------|------|----------------|
| 0:00-0:30 | **The Hook** -- "Every intermission at SOFMC, hundreds of fans walk away from the concession line empty-handed. That's $80K-$120K walking back to their seats every season." | Tension. A problem worth solving. |
| 0:30-1:30 | **The Data** -- Season KPIs. "We analyzed 239K real transactions across 67 games. Here's what we found." Show the per-cap gap ($11.77 vs. $14-16 WHL benchmark). | Credibility. Scale. |
| 1:30-3:00 | **The Discovery** -- Intermission heatmap + Game Explorer on a playoff game. "This heatmap is the smoking gun. Every bright band is a traffic jam. Every gap is money left on the table." | The "aha" moment. |
| 3:00-4:30 | **The Proof of Concept** -- $1 Dog Night deep dive. "One promotion. One dollar. 1,167 hot dog transactions. 30% of all sales that night. We can see EXACTLY what promotions work and replicate them." Then show Stand Performance to reveal which stands are bottlenecks vs. performers. | Concrete evidence. Business impact. |
| 4:30-6:00 | **The Crystal Ball** -- Forecasting page. "Friday night. Vancouver is in town. 4,000 fans expected. Here's EXACTLY what to stock, where to staff, and how much revenue to expect." Show the prep sheet. | Future value. Operational power. |
| 6:00-6:30 | **The Bridge** -- "But a GM doesn't want to click through dashboards. A GM wants to ASK questions and get answers." | Transition to the hero moment. |
| 6:30-9:30 | **The Advisor** -- Live Claude conversation. Three devastating questions. Each one pulls real data and delivers actionable insight. | Jaw drop. |
| 9:30-10:00 | **The Close** -- "This platform turns POS data into revenue decisions. Every game night. For every stand. Starting now." | Confidence. Feasibility. |

### Why This Matters for Judging
Judges score "Business Impact" and "Team Presentation" as two of five criteria. A narrative that connects a dollar problem to a dollar solution through every screen is how you score 10/10 on both.

## 2. Customer Story Gap -- The Missing Fan

This is the PRD's most significant blind spot. The platform is built entirely from the OPERATOR's perspective. Every page answers "what happened?" from a back-office view. No page answers the question: **"What is it like to be a fan at a Royals game, and how does this platform make that experience better?"**

### Where the Fan Should Appear

**In the Intermission Analysis:**
Right now the page shows a transaction heatmap and period bars. Those are operator metrics. The fan story is: "A family of four gets up during the 1st intermission. Dad goes to Island Canteen. The line is 40 people deep. He waits 9 minutes. The period buzzer goes. He walks back empty-handed. His kids don't get their hot dogs. He spent zero dollars." THAT is the story the heatmap should tell. Overlay the chart with a simple annotation or callout: "Estimated 350 fans per intermission abandon the queue. That's $2,800 in lost revenue per game."

**In the Forecasting Page:**
The prep sheet is great for ops. But add one line: "Estimated wait time at Island Canteen: 7.2 minutes. Recommendation: Deploy roaming vendor to reduce to 4 minutes." Now the forecast isn't just about inventory -- it's about the fan experience.

**In the Concession Advisor:**
This is where the fan story comes alive. If the advisor can answer "What does a typical Friday night fan buy?" or "Who's our highest-value customer segment?" -- now the GM is thinking about people, not just product categories.

### The Customer Avatar That Doesn't Exist

The Draft Notes explicitly mention "Who is the main avatar/customer? What do they value?" The PRD never answers this. There should be at least a brief persona or segment reference somewhere in the demo:

- **The Craft Beer Fan**: Comes for Phillips and Stanley Park. Skews 25-40. Spends $18-22/game. Buys beer + premium snack (pretzel, poutine). 80% of all craft sales.
- **The Family**: Comes Saturday. Kids get hot dogs and pop. Parents get beer. Total basket: $35-45. Most price-sensitive segment. Responds to combos and deals.
- **The Student/Young Adult**: Taco Tuesday and $1 Dog Night crowd. Low base spend ($8-12) but high volume on promo nights. Tequila slushy is their Instagram post.

Even if these aren't in the dashboard, the PRESENTER should describe them verbally. The judges need to feel that this team understands who fills those arena seats.

## 4. Branding & Visual Impact

### What "Purple and Gold Royals Theme" Gets You
A nice Streamlit app with colored KPI cards. That's table stakes. Most hackathon teams will have some themed CSS. Purple and gold is fine for branding compliance but it won't make judges lean forward.

### What Would Actually Pop

**The Intermission Heatmap is your visual hero.** If this chart is done well -- tight color gradient, clear time axis, dramatic intermission spikes -- it will be the most memorable image from the entire demo. Make sure the color scale is dramatic: cool blues for quiet periods, hot reds/oranges for intermission crush. This should look like an infrared scan of the arena's revenue heartbeat.

**Big numbers, not small charts.** When showing the per-cap gap, don't put it in a small metric card. Put "$11.77" in 72-point font on the left, "$14-16" on the right, and a big arrow between them labeled "YOUR OPPORTUNITY." Judges sit 10+ feet from the screen. Make them readable from the back row.

**The $1 Dog Night stat deserves a full-screen callout.** When you get to that insight: "1,167 hot dog transactions. 30% of all sales. One promotion." -- that should be a visual moment, not buried in a bar chart. Consider a large annotation or a dedicated callout card.

**The Claude Advisor needs to feel different from the rest of the app.** If every page is charts with purple headers, and then Page 7 is also just a chat box with a purple header, the AI moment doesn't land. Give the Advisor page a distinct visual identity -- a different layout, a spotlight feel, maybe a darker background. It should feel like you're stepping into a different mode of interaction.

**Arena photography as backdrop.** If time permits, even one high-quality photo of SOFMC or a concession stand as a sidebar image or header background transforms the app from "data tool" to "this is a real place with real fans." The team mentioned having photos in a `photos/` folder.

### One More Thing: The First Slide
Before launching Streamlit, the team should have a title slide (even a simple full-screen image in the browser or a single Streamlit page) that shows:
- The team name
- The platform name
- The headline (see Section 9)
- A Royals logo or SOFMC image

First impressions are formed in the first 5 seconds. Don't start by fumbling with a Streamlit sidebar.

## 5. The Promotion Story -- Weaponizing $1 Dog Night

### Why This Data Point Wins the Hackathon

$1 Dog Night is the single most compelling piece of evidence in the entire dataset. It proves that a concession promotion directly drives fan behavior at massive scale. Here is how to use it:

### The Three-Beat $1 Dog Night Story

**Beat 1: The Anomaly**
"When we first looked at the data, one game jumped off the chart. On [date], hot dog transactions spiked to 1,167 -- a 30% share of all transactions that night. Our anomaly detection model flagged this game immediately."

*Show: Game Explorer with the $1 Dog Night selected. The horizontal bar chart will show hot dogs dominating. The anomaly badge should be red/flagged.*

**Beat 2: The Cause**
"That was $1 Dog Night. A simple loss-leader promotion. But look what else happened -- beer sales stayed strong, snack add-ons increased. The promotion didn't cannibalize; it amplified."

*Show: Cross-category sales for that game. If the data supports it (and marketing reports suggest it does), show that beer and complementary items held or grew.*

**Beat 3: The Replication**
"Now ask the Advisor: 'What would happen if we ran $1 Dog Night every other Wednesday?' It can model the demand, estimate the revenue uplift, and flag the inventory requirements. That's the power of turning transaction data into promotional strategy."

*Show: Claude Advisor answering a promotion-related question with real numbers.*

### Other Promotional Insights to Highlight

- **Playoff premium**: "Playoff games produce 25-30% higher per-cap transaction velocity. Fans spend more when the stakes are higher. The platform identifies this so the GM can staff up and stock accordingly."
- **Craft beer dominance**: "Phillips and Stanley Park own 80% of craft sales. That's not just a beverage stat -- it's a partnership negotiation tool. When you sit down with Phillips for next season's sponsorship, you bring these numbers."
- **TacoTacoTaco tequila slushy**: "Low volume, highest per-unit price. This is your Instagram product. The platform identifies these hidden margin champions."
- **ReMax Fan Deck basket size**: "The broadest basket in the arena. Why? Probably because it's a premium section with mobile ordering. That's proof that reducing friction increases spend."

## 6. AI Advisor as Hero -- 5 Killer Demo Questions

The Claude Advisor gets 3 minutes in the demo. That's roughly 3 questions with answers. Pick them carefully. Each question should demonstrate a different capability and deliver a "that's amazing" moment.

### The Five Best Questions (Pick Three for the Demo)

**Question 1: The Dollar Question (Revenue Impact)**
> "How much revenue are we losing during intermissions, and what's the single highest-impact change we could make to capture it?"

Why: This forces the advisor to query the intermission transaction data, calculate the gap, and provide a RECOMMENDATION -- not just a number. It proves the AI can do analysis, not just retrieval. If it says something like "Based on transaction velocity during intermissions and estimated queue abandonment rates, you're leaving an estimated $2,800-$4,000 per high-attendance game on the table. The highest-impact change would be deploying mobile ordering promotion during the 1st period to shift 15-20% of intermission purchases to pre-orders," judges will be impressed.

**Question 2: The Promotion Deep Dive ($1 Dog Night)**
> "Show me the full impact of $1 Dog Night -- did it cannibalize other sales or lift the entire game?"

Why: This is a sophisticated business question. It requires cross-category analysis for a specific game. It tests whether the AI can do nuanced analysis (did cheap hot dogs kill burger and pizza sales, or did the extra foot traffic boost everything?). This directly ties to the promotion story in Section 5.

**Question 3: The Forward-Looking Prediction**
> "We have a Friday game against Vancouver with 4,200 expected fans. How many cases of Phillips Blue Buck should we order, and what's the revenue forecast?"

Why: This activates the forecasting model AND the prep sheet tool. It shows the advisor doing something a dashboard can't -- answering a compound, future-oriented, operational question in natural language. The answer should include specific unit counts and a dollar figure.

**Question 4: The Discovery Question (Hidden Insight)**
> "What's the most surprising pattern in our data that we might be overlooking?"

Why: This is the "wow" question. It asks the AI to surface something non-obvious. If the advisor can identify an anomaly, an unexpected correlation, or an underperforming stand, it demonstrates genuine intelligence rather than canned retrieval. Even if the answer is modest, the question itself signals ambition.

**Question 5: The Strategic Question (GM Perspective)**
> "If Matt Cooke could only make three changes before next season to close the per-cap gap with the WHL average, what should they be?"

Why: This is the money question. It positions the advisor as a strategic consultant, not just a query tool. It forces prioritization, connects to the benchmark gap, and delivers a neat package of recommendations. This should be the LAST question in the demo -- the one that leaves judges thinking "I want that tool."

### Demo Execution Tips
- Pre-load the advisor with conversation history so you don't waste time waiting for cold-start responses.
- Have the questions pre-typed in a notes app so the presenter can paste them quickly.
- If a response is long, narrate over it: "Look -- it's pulling from our demand model AND the transaction database to give a compound answer."
- If something breaks, have a backup screenshot of a successful response.

## 7. What to Cut for Impact

With 10 minutes, every second of dead air or forgettable content is a loss. Here is what to trim:

### Cut or Minimize

| Page/Feature | Recommendation | Reason |
|-------------|----------------|--------|
| **Revenue Analytics (Page 6)** | Cut entirely from demo flow, or show for 10 seconds max | Mock Stripe data is the weakest link. Judges will question synthetic payment data. The revenue story is better told through KPIs and the advisor. |
| **Stand Performance (Page 3)** | Merge the single best insight into Game Explorer | A standalone page of stand comparison charts is forgettable. Take the one killer stat (e.g., "ReMax Fan Deck has the broadest basket") and mention it verbally or show it in the Game Explorer view. |
| **Season KPIs (Page 1)** | Keep, but compress to 30 seconds | Hit three numbers: 239K transactions, $11.77 per-cap (with benchmark comparison), category split. Don't linger on donut charts. |

### Restructured 5-Page Demo Flow

1. **Season KPIs** (30 sec) -- Context and credibility
2. **Game Explorer** (1.5 min) -- $1 Dog Night deep dive as the "proof point"
3. **Intermission Analysis** (1.5 min) -- The heatmap money shot + lost revenue slider
4. **Forecasting** (1.5 min) -- "Plan next Friday's game" interactive moment
5. **Concession Advisor** (3 min) -- Three killer questions

That's 8 minutes of content, leaving 2 minutes for intro, transitions, and close. Much tighter than the original 7-page tour.

### What to Do with Cut Pages
They still exist in the app. If a judge asks "can you show me revenue by payment type?" during Q&A, pull it up. Having it in reserve shows depth. But do not volunteer it during the 10-minute window.

## 8. Missing Marketing Features

The PRD is strong on analytics and operations. It is weak on the features that would make a GM say "I need this for my next sponsorship meeting" or "this changes how I plan promotions." Here are the features that would elevate from "nice analytics tool" to "revenue platform I'd pay for":

### Tier 1: Could Add in 2.5 Hours (Advisor-Powered)

These don't need new dashboard pages -- they can live inside the Claude Advisor's capabilities:

1. **Promotion ROI Calculator**: "If I run $1 Dog Night, what's the estimated cost in margin and the estimated revenue uplift?" The advisor already has the historical data. Prompt it to model this.

2. **Fan Segment Profiles**: Pre-build 3-4 customer segments (Craft Beer Fan, Family, Student, Playoff Power Fan) and let the advisor describe them when asked. This is pure prompt engineering -- no new code needed.

3. **"Next Game" Recommendations**: "What should we do differently for next Friday vs. what we did last Friday?" The advisor can compare two games and suggest tactical changes.

### Tier 2: Would Be Amazing but Probably Out of Scope

4. **Promotion A/B Test Tracker**: Show the impact of promo nights vs. non-promo nights on the same day of week. This might be achievable with a simple filter on the Game Explorer.

5. **Social Media Tie-In Dashboard**: "Which items are most Instagram-worthy?" Based on per-unit price, uniqueness, and margin, rank items by "social media ROI." The tequila slushy wins. This is mostly a curated list -- easy to hardcode.

6. **Sponsorship Value Calculator**: "Phillips represents 60% of craft beer sales. At $X margin per unit, their brand presence at SOFMC is worth $Y in annual revenue." This is powerful for partnership negotiations. Could be a canned advisor response.

7. **"Game Day Checklist" Export**: The forecasting prep sheet formatted as a printable PDF or checklist. Even a simple `st.download_button` with a CSV export of the prep sheet would demonstrate feasibility.

### Tier 3: Future Vision (Mention in the Presentation, Don't Build)

8. **Mobile Ordering Integration**: "Imagine this platform connected to Eventium's mobile ordering. Every pre-order reduces the intermission queue by one transaction."

9. **Dynamic Pricing Engine**: "During high-demand playoff games, the platform could suggest premium pricing for craft beer. During low-attendance weeknights, it triggers flash deals automatically."

10. **Fan Lifetime Value Tracking**: "Combine ticket data with concession data to understand which fans are your highest-value customers across the full season."

### Recommendation for the Next 2.5 Hours
Focus on making the Claude Advisor able to discuss promotion ROI and fan segments when asked. That's prompt engineering, not feature building. It gives you 2-3 extra "wow" answers without writing new dashboard code.

## 9. The Headline

The first slide needs one sentence that frames everything. Here are the options, ranked:

### Top Pick:
> **"Every transaction tells a story. We built the platform that reads them all."**

This works because it's fan-oriented (transactions = people buying things), data-oriented (239K stories), and platform-oriented (we built something). It's also slightly poetic, which is memorable.

### Runner-Up:
> **"Turning 239,000 transactions into the smartest concession stand in the WHL."**

More direct. Leads with the data scale. Implies competitive advantage. Good if the team wants a confident, business-forward tone.

### Aggressive Option:
> **"Your arena is leaving $120K on the table every season. We found it."**

Leads with the problem. Creates tension. Makes judges want to know HOW you found it. High risk, high reward -- only works if the team delivers a confident presentation.

### Safe Option:
> **"AI-Powered Concession Intelligence for the Victoria Royals"**

Clear, descriptive, professional. But it sounds like every other hackathon project. Use this only if the team isn't comfortable with bolder messaging.

### My Recommendation
Go with the Top Pick or the Aggressive Option. Hackathons reward boldness. The "safe" headline won't lose you points, but it won't win you any either.

That moment is the whole demo. Everything else is setup. Make it land.
